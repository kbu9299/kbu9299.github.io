## Topic 1 · The Rise of "Pet Plants" in South Korea

### {{< icon book >}} Reading

In South Korea, a new trend has bloomed in recent years: growing "pet plants" (Ban-ryeo-sik-mul). In the past, people simply bought plants for home decoration or to clean the air. Today, however, many Koreans treat their plants like real pets or family members. They give their plants names, gently wipe their leaves, and even talk to them every morning.

Why are pet plants so popular, especially among people in their 40s and 50s? First, unlike dogs or cats, plants are relatively quiet, cost less money, and do not need to be taken for a walk. Yet, they still provide a great sense of comfort and emotional support. Watching a new green leaf slowly open brings a small but certain joy to our busy and stressful lives. This trend is also connected to "Planterior" (Plant + Interior), making homes look fresh and beautiful.

The culture of pet plants has become so serious that the Korean government and local cities have even opened "plant hospitals." If your favorite plant is turning yellow or looking sick, you can take it to a plant doctor for a check-up and treatment. Taking care of a living thing reminds us to slow down and take better care of ourselves, too.

### {{< icon note >}} Key Vocabulary & Expressions
* bloom: 꽃을 피우다, (비유적으로) 유행하다
* treat A like B: A를 B처럼 대하다
* emotional support: 정서적 지지, 마음의 위안
* small but certain joy: 소확행 (작지만 확실한 행복)
* check-up: 건강 검진, 점검

### {{< icon note >}} Pronunciation Tips
* decoration [ˌdekəˈreɪʃn]: '데코레이션'이라고 할 때 '코'보다 '레이'에 강세를 주어 발음합니다.
* emotional [ɪˈmoʊʃənl]: '이모셔널'에서 o 발음을 굴려 '이-모-셔널'로 부드럽게 이어줍니다.

4. Have you ever failed at growing a plant? Why is it sometimes so hard to keep them alive?
5. If you had a sick plant, would you take it to a "plant hospital"?

## Topic 2 · The Global Shift Towards 'Slow Travel'

### {{< icon book >}} Reading

For decades, the standard approach to an international vacation involved a packed itinerary. Travelers would rush from one famous landmark to another, trying to take pictures in front of ten different monuments in just three days. However, a profound shift is occurring in how people choose to experience the world. Enter the era of "Slow Travel."

Slow travel is not just about the speed of transportation; it is a mindset. It emphasizes deep connection, cultural immersion, and sustainability over ticking boxes on a checklist. Instead of booking tight package tours, slow travelers prefer to rent a local apartment in a quiet neighborhood for a week or a month. They skip the crowded tourist traps and instead buy fresh ingredients from local morning markets, cook their own meals, and observe the daily rhythms of the residents.

For middle-aged travelers who already experience high levels of stress in their professional lives, slow travel offers a genuine escape. It removes the exhausting pressure to "see everything" and replaces it with the freedom to simply "be." Furthermore, this approach is highly beneficial for local economies and the environment, as it reduces carbon footprints and distributes tourism wealth beyond major tourist hotspots. Ultimately, slow travel teaches us that the best memories are often found not in grand museums, but in a quiet chat with a local cafe owner.

### {{< icon note >}} Key Vocabulary & Expressions
* packed itinerary: 꽉 찬 여행 일정표
* profound: 깊은, 심오한, 엄청난
* cultural immersion: 문화적 몰입 (현지 문화에 깊이 빠져드는 것)
* tourist trap: 관광객을 상대로 바가지를 씌우는 곳, 유명하기만 한 관광지
* carbon footprint: 탄소 발자국 (온실가스 배출량)

### {{< icon note >}} Pronunciation Tips
* itinerary [aɪˈtɪnəreri]: 발음이 꽤 까다롭습니다. '아이-티-너-레리'에 가깝게, '티'에 강세를 주어 발음합니다.
* immersion [ɪˈmɜːrʒn]: '이멀젼'보다는 '이-멀-전'에 가깝게 부드럽게 굴려줍니다.
